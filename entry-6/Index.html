Dread

8.5” x 11”

// Mix-Media Zine: Photography, Installation, Acetate Paper, Fish Wire, Pen, Charcoal, Collage, Graphite

In July of 2021, I created a 12-page spread as part of my university art portfolio submission. During this period, I felt a significant change in my relationship with art: A transformation from a source of gratification and leisure to an anxiety-laden process.

Throughout my portfolio-building journey, I aimed to convey a sense of "dread" when creating my artworks, that now revolved around common worries. Such as: What if the reviewer's dislikes my style; What if I do not submit this in time; What if this drawing doesn't turn out how I wanted in time... These are all mundane questions we students throughout my life as an art student, but this feeling exemplified itself during my application period.

I felt as though I had a single opportunity to encapsulate a lifetime of artistic work, skills, and learning.

As one of my final portfolio pieces, I chose to represent this entire experience in a tangible medium: a zine that allows the viewer to flip through my worries. This zine serves as a representation of the feelings of dread and anxiety I've experienced. It was my way of expressing these emotions and momentarily letting go as I approached the conclusion of the portfolio-building process. In my mixed-media zine, I embarked on an art project centered on the theme of change and its connection to the overarching feeling of dread.

Through my lens, I aimed to explore the concept of change and how it often evokes a sense of unease, using various artistic elements like abstraction, mark-making, and editing. A significant part of my creative process involved layering and distorting imagery. I used these techniques to depict the complexity of change, but I also made sure to infuse a sense of dread into these visuals. By overlaying and blending different visual elements, I wanted to represent the mix of emotions and influences that come with significant transformation, some of which can be quite unsettling. Image distortions, in particular, helped me convey the disorienting and even fearful nature of change, emphasizing the sense of dread that it can bring.
